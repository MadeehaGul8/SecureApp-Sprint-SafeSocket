UT2.

Threat name:

Attackers access the system taking advantage of broken authentication

[T-LOGIN-T2]

  • State:

    Exposed

    • Description:

    General Threat Description

    Broken authentication occurs when the implementation of authentication mechanisms in an application or system is flawed, allowing attackers to compromise passwords, keys, or session tokens, or to exploit other implementation flaws to assume other users' identities temporarily or permanently. Common vulnerabilities include inadequate password management, insufficient protection of credentials, and session management flaws.

    Threat Agents/Attack Vectors

    • External Attackers: Individuals or groups targeting the application to gain unauthorized access by exploiting weaknesses in the authentication process.
    • Malicious Insiders: Employees with knowledge of the system's vulnerabilities who might exploit them for personal gain or to cause harm.
    • Automated Attacks: Scripts and bots designed to automatically detect and exploit common authentication vulnerabilities, such as default passwords, weak session tokens, or predictable login mechanisms.

    Impacts

    • Unauthorized Access: Attackers gain unauthorized access to sensitive areas of the application, leading to potential data breaches and unauthorized actions within the system.
    • Data Theft: Once inside, attackers can steal sensitive data, including personal user information, financial records, or proprietary company data.
    • Account Takeover: Attackers can take over user accounts, locking out legitimate users and potentially engaging in identity theft.
    • System Compromise: Gaining initial access through broken authentication can be a stepping stone for attackers to explore further vulnerabilities within the system, leading to a more extensive compromise.

    Example Attack Scenarios

    Scenario 1: An attacker uses a list of common passwords to perform a brute-force attack on a web application’s login page. Due to the absence of account lockout policies, the attacker successfully guesses a user's password, gaining unauthorized access to sensitive information.

    Scenario 2: A developer inadvertently leaves a default administrator account enabled in a production environment, with credentials that are easily found in public documentation. An attacker discovers this account and uses it to gain full administrative access to the application.

    Scenario 3: An application's session tokens are not securely generated and are susceptible to prediction. An attacker crafts a session token through analysis of multiple tokens and successfully hijacks a user's authenticated session, gaining access without needing the account's password.

    Mitigation Strategies

    • Implement Strong Authentication Mechanisms: Utilize multi-factor authentication (MFA) to add an additional layer of security beyond usernames and passwords.
    • Secure Password Storage: Store passwords securely using salted hashing techniques. Ensure that the storage mechanism is resistant to brute-force attacks.
    • Session Management Security: Generate secure, random session tokens and ensure that session tokens expire after a reasonable period of inactivity or are invalidated after logout.
    • Account Lockout Policies: Implement account lockout policies after a certain number of unsuccessful login attempts to prevent brute-force attacks.
    • Regular Security Audits and Testing: Conduct regular security audits and penetration testing to identify and remediate vulnerabilities related to authentication mechanisms.
    • Educate Users on Secure Practices: Encourage users to use strong, unique passwords and to be mindful of security practices related to authentication.

    By understanding and addressing the risks associated with broken authentication, organizations can significantly enhance the security of their login systems and protect against unauthorized access and data breaches.

    RECOMMENDED

UT3.

Threat name:

Attackers exploit flaws in access control systems

[T-LOGIN-T1]

  • State:

    Exposed

    • Description:

    General Threat Description

    Access control systems are designed to restrict access to resources in a computing environment to authorized users, services, or processes. These systems can be compromised by attackers exploiting flaws, leading to unauthorized access, data breaches, system manipulation, or denial of access to legitimate users. Flaws might include inadequate password management, improper configuration, lack of proper updates, and vulnerabilities within the access control software itself.

    Threat Agents/Attack Vectors

    • Insider Threats: Employees or insiders with malicious intent or negligence exploiting their knowledge of the system.
    • Cybercriminals: Individuals or groups seeking unauthorized access for financial gain, espionage, or other malicious purposes.
    • Hacktivists: Attackers motivated by political or social causes aiming to disrupt services or expose vulnerabilities.
    • Automated Bots: Scripts and bots that automatically scan for vulnerabilities to exploit in access control systems.

    Impacts

    • Data Breach: Unauthorized access could lead to exposure of sensitive, confidential, or personally identifiable information.
    • Service Disruption: Critical services may be disrupted, affecting business operations and causing financial and reputational damage.
    • System Compromise: Attackers might gain control over systems, potentially leading to a wider network compromise.
    • Compliance Violations: Breaches in access control could result in violations of regulatory requirements, leading to legal penalties and fines.

    Example Attack Scenarios

    Brute Force Attacks: An attacker uses a brute force approach to guess passwords and gain unauthorized access to a system. By exploiting weak password policies, the attacker successfully logs in as an administrator and accesses sensitive data.

    Exploiting Configuration Errors: A misconfigured access control list allows unauthorized users to access restricted directories. An attacker discovers this flaw and downloads confidential documents, leading to a data breach.

    Session Hijacking: An attacker exploits a flaw in the session management system to take over a legitimate user session after authentication. This allows the attacker to perform unauthorized actions under the guise of a legitimate user.

    Permission Bypass: Due to a flaw in the access control logic, attackers bypass the usual permission checks and gain access to functionalities reserved for higher-privileged users, enabling them to manipulate system settings or access restricted data.

    Mitigating these threats requires a multifaceted approach, including regular security assessments, robust access control policies, user education, and prompt patching of identified vulnerabilities.

UT4.

Threat name:

Attackers get access to sensitive data

[T-LOGIN-T3]

  • State:

    Exposed

    • Description:

    General Threat Description

    This threat involves unauthorized access to sensitive information, such as personal user data, financial details, or proprietary business information, through vulnerabilities in login systems. These vulnerabilities might include weak authentication mechanisms, insecure data transmission, and insufficient data protection practices. Once attackers bypass these defenses, they can access, exfiltrate, and potentially exploit sensitive data.

    Threat Agents/Attack Vectors

    • External Attackers: Cybercriminals targeting the application to steal sensitive data for financial gain, identity theft, or competitive advantage.
    • Malicious Insiders: Employees or contractors exploiting their access to login systems and related data for unauthorized purposes.
    • Phishing Attacks: Attackers tricking users into revealing their login credentials through deceptive emails or fake websites.
    • Credential Stuffing: Automated attacks using breached username and password pairs to gain unauthorized access to user accounts.

    Impacts

    • Data Breach: The unauthorized access and extraction of sensitive data can lead to significant breaches, affecting individuals and organizations.
    • Financial Loss: Direct financial loss can result from the theft of financial information or through fraudulent transactions made using stolen credentials.
    • Reputation Damage: Incidents of data exposure can severely damage the reputation of an organization, leading to lost trust among users and customers.
    • Regulatory Penalties: Many jurisdictions have stringent data protection regulations. Non-compliance due to data exposure can lead to legal penalties and fines.

    Example Attack Scenarios

    Scenario 1: An attacker conducts a successful phishing campaign, obtaining login credentials for a financial application. Using these credentials, they access sensitive customer financial data, leading to identity theft and unauthorized transactions.

    Scenario 2: Exploiting weak password policies and the lack of multi-factor authentication (MFA) on a corporate login system, attackers use a list of common passwords to gain access to employee accounts, through which they exfiltrate proprietary business information.

    Scenario 3: Through a man-in-the-middle (MitM) attack, an attacker intercepts unencrypted login requests between users and a web application. Capturing usernames and passwords, the attacker gains access to the application, retrieving personal data stored within user profiles.

    Mitigation Strategies

    • Implement Strong Authentication: Utilize multi-factor authentication (MFA) and strong password policies to enhance the security of login systems.
    • Secure Communications: Use HTTPS with TLS for all data in transit, including login sessions, to prevent data interception.
    • Regular Security Audits: Conduct periodic security audits and penetration testing to identify and remediate vulnerabilities in login systems.
    • User Education: Educate users on the importance of secure password practices and the risks of phishing and other social engineering attacks.
    • Credential Protection: Store credentials securely using salted hashing techniques. Regularly review and update security practices related to credential storage and handling.
    • Anomaly Detection and Monitoring: Implement systems to detect and alert on unusual access patterns or login attempts that could indicate a breach attempt.

    By understanding and addressing the threat of attackers gaining access to sensitive data through login systems, organizations can significantly reduce the risk of data breaches and protect the privacy and security of user information.

UT5.

Threat name:

Attackers capitalize on security misconfigurations

[T-LOGIN-T4]

  • State:

    Exposed

    • Description:

    General Threat Description

    Security misconfigurations can occur at any level of an IT stack, including network devices, operating systems, applications, and cloud services. These misconfigurations often stem from default settings, incomplete setups, overly permissive access controls, unnecessary services running, or outdated security controls. Attackers exploit these vulnerabilities to gain unauthorized access, escalate privileges, or execute malicious actions within a system.

    Threat Agents/Attack Vectors

    • External Attackers: Individuals or groups outside the organization who exploit misconfigurations to gain initial access or escalate their privileges within a network.
    • Insider Threats: Employees or contractors who exploit internal system misconfigurations for malicious purposes or personal gain.
    • Automated Tools and Scripts: Attackers often use automated tools to scan for common misconfigurations across internet-facing services.

    Impacts

    • Unauthorized Access: Attackers may gain unauthorized access to sensitive systems or data, leading to information theft or data breaches.
    • System Compromise: Exploiting misconfigurations can allow attackers to plant malware, create backdoors, or gain control over critical systems.
    • Regulatory and Compliance Violations: Security misconfigurations leading to breaches may result in non-compliance with legal and regulatory standards, potentially incurring fines and legal action.
    • Reputational Damage: Incidents resulting from misconfigurations can damage an organization's reputation, affecting customer trust and market position.

    Example Attack Scenarios

    Default Credentials: Attackers exploit default credentials on a database server to gain unauthorized access. They extract sensitive data, leading to a significant data breach.

    Open Cloud Storage Buckets: An organization leaves a cloud storage bucket misconfigured as publicly accessible. Attackers discover this bucket and exfiltrate sensitive documents, exposing personal data of thousands of users.

    Unsecured Administrative Interfaces: A network device is left with an administrative interface accessible over the internet without proper authentication. Attackers gain control of the device, using it as a pivot point for further internal network exploitation.

    Excessive Permissions: Due to misconfigured role-based access controls, users have more permissions than necessary for their role, allowing an attacker to exploit a user account to perform unauthorized actions within a system.

    To mitigate these threats, organizations must adopt a proactive approach to security configuration management, including regular audits, adherence to security best practices, least privilege access, prompt patch management, and continuous monitoring for unauthorized changes.

Threat name:

Attackers intercept and tamper with data

[T-DATA-INTERCEPT]

  • State:

    Exposed

    • Description:

    General Threat Description

    Attackers intercepting and tampering with data involve unauthorized access to and alteration of the communication between a client and a web server. This threat exploits vulnerabilities in the web server, its configuration, or the network, allowing attackers to read, modify, or redirect data. The lack of encryption or improper encryption practices often facilitates these attacks, significantly undermining the confidentiality and integrity of the data exchanged.

    Threat Agents/Attack Vectors

    The primary threat agents include cybercriminals, espionage groups, and malicious insiders, utilizing techniques such as Man-in-the-Middle (MitM) attacks, packet sniffing, and exploiting server vulnerabilities. Attack vectors can range from compromised network equipment, such as routers, to software vulnerabilities in the web server itself or the protocols it uses. Attackers may also leverage unsecured or poorly secured Wi-Fi networks to intercept communications.

    Impacts

    The impacts of data interception and tampering can be severe, leading to the compromise of sensitive information, such as user credentials, financial data, and personal information. It can also result in the dissemination of malware, the alteration of transaction data, and loss of trust among users and clients. Furthermore, such attacks could have legal and regulatory implications for the affected organization, especially if it leads to a breach of data protection laws.

    Example Attack Scenarios
    • An attacker performing a MitM attack intercepts and alters the communication between a user's browser and an online banking website, redirecting a transaction to a different account.
    • Through packet sniffing on an unencrypted Wi-Fi network, a cybercriminal captures login credentials that a user submits to a web application, leading to unauthorized access to the user’s account.
    • Exploiting vulnerabilities in the web server’s configuration, an attacker modifies the contents of a message being sent from the client to the server, injecting malicious code that compromises the server.
    • An attacker intercepts data being transmitted over an insecure connection and inserts a malicious redirect into the session, leading users to a phishing site designed to capture their credentials.

UT11.

Threat name:

Attackers manipulate SSL/TLS weaknesses, e.g., Heartbleed

[T-SSL-TLS-W]

  • State:

    Exposed

    • Description:

    General Threat Description

    SSL/TLS weaknesses exploit vulnerabilities in the implementation or configuration of the encryption protocols used to secure internet communications. These weaknesses can lead to various security issues, such as the decryption, modification, and interception of supposed-to-be secure data. Notable vulnerabilities like Heartbleed have highlighted the risks associated with outdated or improperly configured encryption protocols and underscored the importance of using up-to-date and strong ciphers, libraries, and configurations.

    Threat Agents/Attack Vectors

    Threat agents include nation-states, cybercriminal organizations, and individual hackers interested in intercepting or tampering with encrypted data. The attack vectors vary but often involve exploiting known vulnerabilities in specific versions of SSL/TLS protocols or their implementations, such as OpenSSL. Attackers may also leverage weaknesses in cryptographic practices, such as the use of weak or deprecated encryption ciphers.

    Impacts

    The impacts of exploiting SSL/TLS weaknesses can be profound, affecting the confidentiality and integrity of communications. This may lead to sensitive information being exposed, such as login credentials, personal data, or financial information. It can also undermine the trust users place in the security of online transactions and communications, potentially leading to financial loss and reputational damage for the entities involved.

    Example Attack Scenarios
    • An attacker exploits the Heartbleed vulnerability in an outdated version of OpenSSL to extract data from the memory of a web server, including private keys, user passwords, and personal information.
    • Through the use of a weak cipher, an attacker performs a man-in-the-middle attack, decrypting and altering data being exchanged between a client and a server without detection.
    • An attacker exploits vulnerabilities in the SSLv3 protocol, such as POODLE, to downgrade the encryption security and intercept the data being transferred over what is assumed to be a secure connection.
  • C8.Countermeasure name: Implement strong and up-to-date cipher suites

UT12.

Threat name:

Attackers manipulate the server in unintended ways, e.g., injection attacks

[T-MANIPULATE-SYSTEM]

  • State:

    Exposed

    • Description:

    General Threat Description

    Injection attacks allow attackers to manipulate a server by executing unauthorized commands or accessing data through the server's interfaces. These attacks exploit vulnerabilities in the server's input validation mechanisms to inject malicious code or commands, leading to a wide range of malicious activities. The most common forms include SQL injection, Cross-Site Scripting (XSS), Command Injection, and Remote File Inclusion (RFI).

    Threat Agents/Attack Vectors

    Threat agents include cybercriminals, hacking groups, and sometimes malicious insiders with varying levels of technical expertise. The primary attack vectors involve submitting crafted input into web forms, query parameters, or any input fields that are improperly sanitized by the server. This can be achieved via web applications, API endpoints, or any interface that processes user-supplied data.

    Impacts

    The impacts of injection attacks are severe and varied, ranging from unauthorized access to sensitive data, complete system compromise, denial of service, to the exposure of confidential information. These attacks can lead to significant financial losses, damage to reputation, and legal consequences, especially if they result in the breach of data protection regulations.

    Example Attack Scenarios
    • An attacker uses an RFI vulnerability to include a malicious file from a remote server, executing a script that gives them unauthorized access to the web server's file system.
    • Through a SQL injection attack, a cybercriminal manipulates a query form on a website to gain access to the database, extracting sensitive user information, including passwords and personal data.
    • Exploiting a path traversal flaw, an attacker manipulates the web application to access files and directories stored outside the web root folder, leading to the exposure of sensitive configuration files.
    • An attacker sends crafted inputs that cause the application to generate an error message, revealing details about the underlying database structure, which they then exploit to perform more targeted attacks.

UT6.

Threat name:

Attackers cause a service disruption, e.g., DoS

[T-DISRUPT-NORMAL-OPERATIONS]

  • State:

    Exposed

    • Description:

    General Threat Description

    Service disruption attacks, including Denial of Service (DoS), aim to make a web server unavailable to its intended users by overwhelming it with a flood of requests or by exploiting its resource management weaknesses. This type of attack targets the availability aspect of security, one of the core components of the CIA triad (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability).

    Threat Agents/Attack Vectors

    Threat agents include individual hackers, activist groups, or state-sponsored entities using various attack vectors such as botnets to launch distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) attacks, or exploiting software vulnerabilities to exhaust server resources. These agents can also perform more sophisticated attacks by exploiting specific vulnerabilities in the server's operating system or applications to cause a denial of service.

    Impacts

    The impacts of service disruption attacks can be significant, ranging from temporary unavailability of the service, which can lead to loss of revenue and trust among users, to severe resource exhaustion, causing a complete shutdown of the server. In some cases, these attacks can also serve as a smokescreen for more malicious activities, such as data breaches.

    Example Attack Scenarios
    • An attacker employs a botnet to send an overwhelming number of requests to a web server, causing a denial of service for legitimate users.
    • Exploiting a vulnerability in the server’s application, an attacker causes the server to allocate excessive memory for certain requests, eventually leading to system crash due to memory exhaustion.
    • An attacker discovers an application flaw that allows them to create an infinite loop, consuming the server's CPU resources and rendering the service unavailable.
    • Through a flaw in the server’s operating system, an attacker repeatedly opens files without closing them, eventually depleting the available file descriptors and preventing the server from serving legitimate requests.

UT7.

Threat name:

Attackers carry out cross-service attacks

[T-CROSS-SERVICE]

  • State:

    Exposed

    • Description:

    General Threat Description

    Cross-service attacks exploit vulnerabilities or misconfigurations in one service to compromise or affect another within the same network or environment. These attacks leverage the interconnectedness and interdependencies of services in a modern computing environment, targeting the lack of proper segmentation, insecure service integrations, or misconfigurations. The ultimate goal of such attacks is often to 'pivot' within the environment, escalating access from less sensitive to more sensitive areas.

    Threat Agents/Attack Vectors

    Threat agents can range from external attackers to malicious insiders. They use a variety of attack vectors, including exploiting known vulnerabilities in exposed services, taking advantage of insufficient network segmentation, or leveraging stolen credentials to move laterally across services. Other common vectors include the use of phishing attacks to gain initial access or the exploitation of insecure APIs and service interfaces for lateral movement.

    Impacts

    The impacts of cross-service attacks can be widespread, affecting multiple services and components within the same environment. These can include data breaches, loss of data integrity, service disruption, and unauthorized access to sensitive information. The ability for attackers to pivot across services also increases the potential for more significant damage, as they can leverage the access gained from one compromised service to attack more secure or sensitive areas within the network.

    Example Attack Scenarios
    • An attacker exploits a vulnerability in a web server to gain initial access to the network, then uses this foothold to exploit insecure API calls between services to access a database containing sensitive information.
    • Through a misconfigured network, an attacker moves laterally from a compromised external-facing service to an internal service that is not exposed to the internet, leading to unauthorized access to internal-only data and operations.

Threat name:

Attackers gain access to information about the server, e.g., verbose errors or misconfiguration

[T-INFORMATION-LEAKAGE]

  • State:

    Exposed

    • Description:

    General Threat Description

    This threat involves attackers gaining unauthorized access to sensitive information about the server, such as software versions, configuration details, and even secrets like passwords or encryption keys. This can occur through various means, including verbose error messages, misconfigurations, unsecured network communications, and vulnerable server functions. The exposure of such information can significantly weaken the security posture of the server, making it more susceptible to targeted attacks.

    Threat Agents/Attack Vectors

    Threat agents can include external attackers, malicious insiders, or automated bots scanning for vulnerabilities. Attack vectors involve exploiting misconfigured server settings that expose too much information, inducing errors that reveal sensitive information, intercepting unencrypted traffic to read data transmitted between the server and its clients, and exploiting vulnerabilities in the server's functions that may leak information about its internal state or operations.

    Impacts

    The impacts of information leakage are multi-faceted. They can lead to a loss of confidentiality, enabling attackers to conduct more targeted and effective attacks. For instance, knowledge about specific software versions can allow attackers to exploit known vulnerabilities. Additionally, the exposure of sensitive information can lead to compliance issues, legal consequences, and a loss of trust from users and clients.

    Example Attack Scenarios
    • An attacker sends unexpected input to the server, triggering a verbose error message that includes database connection details or file paths on the server.
    • Through misconfigured HTTP headers, an attacker discovers the exact version of the web server software being used, enabling them to exploit known vulnerabilities specific to that version.
    • An attacker captures unencrypted traffic between the server and a client, gaining access to sensitive information transmitted in plaintext, such as user credentials or personal data.
    • Exploiting a memory vulnerability in the server application, an attacker is able to read parts of the server memory that contain sensitive information, such as user session tokens or secret keys.
  • C12.Countermeasure name: Secure configuration and server hardening

  • State:

    RECOMMENDED

UT9.

Threat name:

Insufficient logging

[T-INSUFFICIENT-LOGGING]

  • State:

    Exposed

    • Description:

    General Threat Description

    Insufficient logging is a critical security oversight where an environment fails to adequately record events, or the logs lack the necessary detail to identify and understand security incidents. This deficiency hampers the detection of malicious activities, compromises the identification of attack patterns, and undermines forensic analysis capabilities. It results in prolonged detection and response times to security incidents, thereby increasing the risk and potential impact of breaches.

    Threat Agents/Attack Vectors

    Threat agents exploiting insufficient logging range from external hackers to malicious insiders. Without comprehensive logging, these actors can operate undetected within an environment, exploiting vulnerabilities and extracting sensitive information. The lack of detailed logs also means that even after detection, tracing the attackers' steps and understanding the full scope of the compromise is challenging, if not impossible.

    Impacts

    The primary impact of insufficient logging is the extended dwell time of attackers within a network, increasing the potential for damage and data loss. It also significantly hinders incident response and forensic investigations, leading to delayed responses and potentially incorrect assessments of incidents. In the long term, insufficient logging can lead to regulatory and compliance issues, reputational damage, and financial losses due to the inability to defend against or quickly respond to attacks.

    Example Attack Scenarios
    • An attacker exploits a vulnerability in a web application to gain unauthorized access to the system. Due to insufficient logging, the intrusion goes unnoticed for an extended period, allowing the attacker to extract sensitive data undetected.
    • A malicious insider abuses their access rights to exfiltrate company data. The lack of detailed access and activity logs prevents the identification of the insider as the source of the breach.
    • Due to insufficient logging, a company fails to notice the indicators of a ransomware attack in its early stages. This oversight allows the ransomware to encrypt critical data and spread across the network, leading to significant operational disruption.
    • In a cloud environment, the default logging settings are not adjusted to capture detailed event logs. As a result, when an attacker compromises cloud-based resources, the attack goes undetected for weeks, causing extensive data loss and system compromise.
  • C13.Countermeasure name: Ensure proper logging and monitoring

  • State:

    RECOMMENDED
